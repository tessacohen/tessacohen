Becoming the Cubs

The story of the Chicago Cubs that we know and love today begins in 1876. Chicago’s most beloved team joined the National Association of Professional Baseball, under the name of the White Stockings. They quickly became considered one of the earliest powerhouses in the league after winning 6 titles in their first 11 years of participation.

A key player from this era was Adrian “Cap” Anson. His record breaking career and dedication to the team led him to hold a management position for a total of 19 years. He got his nickname, “Cap” from his captain and father-like relaitonship that he had with the team.

After more than several successful seasons, the team changed their name to the Colts from 1890 to 1897. The Colts was changed to the Orphans in 1897 when “Cap” Anson retired from working for the franchise. That same year, the Chicago Daily News referred to them as the “Cubs.” This nickname came from the young age of majority of the team. It stuck with them until 1907 when it was adopted as the official name.

1907

The Cubs then set a record for 116 wins, while in the same year, lost the World Series in game 6. The following season, in 1907, they won 107 games and went on to win the World Series against the Detroit Tigers. The following year, they won their second World Series title.
